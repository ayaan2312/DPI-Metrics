What is DPI Metrics?

DPI Metrics is a free browser-based tool that lets you measure the real-world performance of any computer mouse — no software to install, no account required. All processing happens locally in your browser.

What is Mouse Polling Rate (Hz)?

Polling rate is how frequently your mouse reports its position to your computer, measured in Hertz (Hz). A 125 Hz mouse sends position updates 125 times per second; a 1000 Hz mouse sends 1000. Higher polling rates mean lower input latency and smoother cursor tracking — critical in competitive gaming where 1 ms can matter.

Most gaming mice today offer 125 Hz, 500 Hz, or 1000 Hz polling rates. Some flagship mice now reach 4000 Hz or even 8000 Hz, though diminishing returns set in above 1000 Hz for most use cases.

What is Mouse DPI?

DPI stands for Dots Per Inch — it measures how many pixels your cursor moves on screen for every physical inch your mouse moves. A mouse set to 800 DPI will move the cursor 800 pixels for every inch of physical movement. Higher DPI = more sensitive, faster cursor. Lower DPI = more precise, slower cursor.

The "best" DPI is personal preference, but most professional FPS gamers prefer 400–800 DPI combined with a high in-game sensitivity multiplier, as it reduces sensor noise and jitter. The DPI Calibration tab lets you measure your mouse's actual DPI — not just what the software claims.

What is Sensor Jitter?

Jitter is the small, random deviations in your mouse's reported position that don't correspond to your actual hand movement. It's caused by the optical sensor's noise floor, the surface texture, and the sensor's firmware smoothing algorithms. The Jitter Plot tab visualizes this by measuring how much your actual path deviates from the ideal straight line you intended to draw.

Lower jitter means more precise tracking. This is measured as RMS (root mean square) deviation in pixels. Under 0.5px RMS is excellent; above 2px suggests either a poor surface or sensor acceleration issues.

Browser Measurement Limitations

This tool uses the Web PointerEvent API with getCoalescedEvents() where available (Chromium 58+, Firefox 59+) to access intermediate mouse samples between display frames. Without it, browsers coalesce events to the display refresh rate, which means a 1000 Hz mouse may appear to report at ~240 Hz on a 240 Hz monitor. For true hardware-level polling verification, a native OS tool is needed — but this tool is the most accurate browser-based measurement available.

Privacy

All mouse data is processed entirely in your browser's memory using JavaScript. No coordinates, timings, or any other data are ever transmitted to a server. The source code is straightforward and auditable.
